Technical Reference

The formulas and mechanics that support the Extended TRS framework.

1. Return Construction

1.1 TRS as the Baseline

TRS remains the market reference. It captures realised shareholder outcomes and applies universally. Total Return to Shareholders remains the baseline reference for financial performance. It anchors market accountability and comparability.

Total Return to Shareholders
TRS = P1 − P0 + DP0 × 100%
Where:
  • P₁ = ending share price
  • P₀ = beginning share price
  • D = dividends paid during period
1.2 TR²S - Total Regenerative Return to Shareholders

TR²S extends return interpretation to internal asset allocation, lifetime extension, and revenue structure. It applies across sectors and ownership models. Its focus is internal: how assets are configured, preserved, and redeployed over time.

Total Regenerative Return to Shareholders
TR²S = TRS × RM  (when TRS ≥ 0)
TR²S = TRS × (1 − θ · RM)  (when TRS < 0)
Where:
  • RM = Regenerative Multiplier (asset allocation, lifetime extension, revenue structure effects)
When TRS ≥ or < 0

The Regenerative Multiplier (RM) amplifies upside and dampens downside.

When TRS ≥ 0

TR²S = TRS × RM

The Regenerative Multiplier acts as an amplifier. Internal regeneration creates additional value capture beyond market returns.

When TRS < 0

TR²S = TRS × (1 − θ · RM)

When market returns are negative, the downside damping coefficient (θ) limits the negative impact of the Regenerative Multiplier. This prevents compounding losses.

Where:
  • θ (theta) is the downside coefficient applied only when TRS is negative

RM - Regenerative Multiplier

The Regenerative Multiplier is a weighted combination of three key operational inputs that reflect how well a company manages its assets, extends their productive life, and circulates revenue internally.

Regenerative Multiplier Formula
RM = α · rar + β · lve + γ · crr
Components:
  • RAR (Regenerative Asset Ratio) = Regenerative Assets / Total Assets
  • LVE (Lifetime Value Extension) = Extended Lifetime / Baseline Lifetime
  • CRR (Circular Revenue Ratio) = Circular Revenue / Total Revenue
Weights:
  • α (alpha) = weight on RAR
  • β (beta) = weight on LVE
  • γ (gamma) = weight on CRR

1.3 TR³S - Total Regenerative & Restorative Return to Shareholders

TR³S extends return interpretation to external ecosystem restoration and social regeneration. It applies to companies with direct extractive exposure and measures how operations contribute to environmental and social healing, beyond internal circular processes.

Total Regenerative & Restorative Return to Shareholders
TR³S = TR²S × (1 + RE)
Where:
  • RE = Restoration Effect (external ecosystem and social regeneration impact)

Restoration Effect (RE) Components

Restoration Effect Formula
RE = PA × RI     RI = λ · VCI + μ · ECR
Components:
  • PA (Purpose Alignment) = degree to which company strategy aligns with environmental/social restoration (−1 to +1)
  • VCI (Value Chain Impact) = direct regenerative impact within supply chain (0–1)
  • ECR (Ecosystem Restoration) = direct investment in ecosystem recovery outside supply chain (0–1)
  • λ (lambda) = weight on VCI
  • μ (mu) = weight on ECR

2. Capital Eligibility

Extended return logic may inform long-term incentives only when the company is financially capable of sustaining asset quality and long-term investment. Capital eligibility therefore precedes all vesting calculations. It functions as a governance gate, not as a performance input: if the gate is closed, extended return logic (TR²S, TR³S) cannot be used in LTI.

2.1 The Free Gate - Capital Efficiency Gatekeepers

Before extended return logic can inform compensation, governance must first establish that the company is financially sound. The Free Gate consists of three capital-efficiency gatekeepers that determine whether asset preservation is economically viable or whether the company is in financial distress. These gates are not performance targets but thresholds: they answer the question, “Is this company capable of investing in asset quality, or is it in survival mode?”

Gate 1: ROIC ≥ WACC

This gate tests whether the company creates or destroys value from its capital base. ROIC must exceed WACC by a governance-set margin; if ROIC ≤ WACC, capital is not rewarded adequately and asset preservation becomes secondary to financial stabilisation.

ROIC = NOPATInvested Capital
WACC = (EV × Re) + (DV × Rd × (1 − Tc))

ROIC exceeds WACC: Company is creating value. Asset preservation investments are economically justified.

ROIC ≤ WACC: Company is destroying value. Asset preservation is secondary to financial stabilisation.

Gate 2: FCF Conversion - Free Cash Flow Supports Investment

FCF must be structurally positive and convert earnings into cash at a healthy ratio; if FCF is negative or conversion is weak, asset-preservation investments are deferred.

FCF = Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditures
FCF Conversion = FCFNet Income

FCF > 0 and Conversion 80–120%: Earnings are backed by cash. Gate 2 PASSED.

FCF ≤ 0 or Conversion < 50%: Company is in cash stress. Asset preservation is deferred.

Gate 3: Leverage Ratio

Net leverage must be within a governance-set sector cap; if leverage is excessive, debt reduction takes priority over regenerative investment.

Net DebtEBITDA

Net Debt/EBITDA ≤ Sector Cap (e.g., 3.0×): Leverage is sustainable. Gate 3 PASSED.

Net Debt/EBITDA exceeds Sector Cap: Leverage is excessive. Asset preservation is deferred.

Summary: When All Gates Are Passed

Only when all three gates are passed is the company deemed financially free to allocate capital toward regenerative and restorative configurations.

2.2 Capital Eligibility Status (CES)

CES condenses the Free Gate into a single status variable:

CES = 1

All three gates passed. Extended return logic (TR²S, TR³S) is admissible as input into LTI vesting.

CES = 0

One or more gates failed. Extended return logic is not used for LTI; payouts are reduced to a distress cap or set to zero.

CES is determined mechanically from the three gates and carried forward into vesting mechanics as a binary switch.

2.3 Gate Threshold Disclosure

All gate thresholds are set ex-ante and disclosed. For each gate, governance specifies:

  • The minimum ROIC–WACC spread required (for example, ROIC ≥ WACC + 2–3 percentage points).
  • The minimum acceptable range for FCF conversion and any temporary tolerance during investment cycles.
  • Sector-specific caps for Net Debt/EBITDA and any complementary leverage indicators used.

These parameters are governance choices, not optimisation outputs. Changes follow a formal decision process and are documented.

3. Compensation Architecture

Extended return interpretation matters only when it is reflected in long-term incentives. As long as long-term incentives are tied exclusively to traditional shareholder return, governance systematically favours timing effects over internal allocation quality.

3.1 Baseline Compensation Architecture

The extended return framework does not redesign executive compensation. It keeps the underlying architecture intact and changes only the performance input used in the LTI formula.

  • Base salary
  • Short-term incentive (STI) structure and inputs
  • Long-term incentive (LTI) target opportunity
  • Performance ladder form (0 / 50 / 100 / 150 / 200% of target)
  • Caps and floors on payouts

Extended return logic operates only through the choice of return input that feeds the LTI.

3.3 Input Selection for LTI

Before any LTI payout is calculated, governance must fix the return input and the ladder parameters ex-ante.

  1. Select the return input used for the performance cycle (TRS, TR²S, or TR³S).
  2. Confirm whether the standard 0 / 50 / 100 / 150 / 200% ladder applies unchanged.
  3. Set threshold, target, stretch and cap levels for the chosen input.
  4. Decide whether TR³S is applicable for the cycle (based on structural extractive exposure).
  5. Define the point at which STI results are locked and no longer adjusted.

4. Vesting Mechanics

Vesting is the mechanism through which governance translates return signals into incentive outcomes. Grants are made at target. Final payouts depend on capital eligibility, return performance, and predefined vesting rules.

4.1 LTI Grant

The long-term incentive target is expressed as a fixed percentage of base salary.

LTI target (EUR) = Base Salary × LTI multiple
Shares granted = LTI targetShare Price at Grant
4.2 Base Vesting Factor (BVF)

The Base Vesting Factor (BVF) reflects return performance under the input selected by governance for the cycle.

BVF = g(Return input)

Where g() represents the performance ladder defined ex ante. BVF ∈ {0%, 50%, 100%, 150%, 200%}. Interpolation between ladder points is linear unless otherwise specified by governance.

4.3 Regenerative Adjustment (RA)

The Regenerative Adjustment (RA) applies only when TR²S or TR³S is selected as the return input.

RA = 1 + λ × (RM − RMref)
Where:
  • λ is a governance-set sensitivity parameter
  • RM_ref is the sector median or governance-defined baseline
RAmin ≤ RA ≤ RAmax

The regenerative adjustment refines the base vesting factor. It cannot override capital eligibility conditions.

4.4 Final Vesting Factor (FVF), with Price-Floor Governance Rule

Price-Floor Governance Rule

Regardless of the calculated TRS value (which includes dividends), no vesting occurs if the share price at the end of the performance period is below the starting share price.

If P1 < P0 : FVF = 0

This governance filter ensures that executives do not receive LTI payouts when shareholder value has declined, even if dividend income results in a technically positive TRS. It anchors vesting to capital appreciation rather than income distribution alone.

Standard FVF Formula (applies only if P₁ ≥ P₀):

FVF = min [ BVF × RA × CES , Cap ]
Where:
  • BVF = Base Vesting Factor
  • RA = Regenerative Adjustment
  • CES = Capital Eligibility Status (0, 0.5, or 1)
  • Cap = LTI cap (e.g., 200% of target)

If CES = 0

FVF = 0

If CES = 0.5

FVF is capped regardless of return performance

If CES = 1

Vesting proceeds without restriction under the selected return input

If P₁ < P₀: FVF = 0 for all return inputs (TRS, TR²S, TR³S), regardless of CES or calculated return value.

Example: Maersk 2024

Given: P₀ = 12,385 DKK  |  P₁ = 11,905 DKK  |  D = 1,120 DKK


Calculated TRS: (11,905 − 12,385 + 1,120) / 12,385 = +5.2%


Governance Filter: P₁ (11,905) < P₀ (12,385) → FVF = 0%


Result: Despite a positive TRS of +5.2% (due to dividend compensation), the price-floor governance rule triggers zero vesting because the share price declined.

5. LTI Payout

Once the Final Vesting Factor is determined, payout calculation is purely mechanical. No discretion applies.

5.1 Vested Shares

The number of shares that vest is determined by applying the Final Vesting Factor to the originally granted shares.

Vested shares = Granted shares × FVF100
5.2 Monetary Value at Vesting

The monetary value of vested shares is calculated using the share price at the vesting date.

LTI payout value = Vested shares × Pvesting

6. Governance Lock

All calculations are deterministic once governance decisions are taken ex ante. The framework distinguishes between fixed parameters and parameters subject to periodic governance review.

6.1 Change-Control Rules

Fixed Parameters (No Changes During Grant Cycle)

  • Ladder structure (0%, 50%, 100%, 150%, 200%)
  • Performance cap (e.g., 200% of target)
  • Vesting schedule and cliff dates
  • Share price measurement methodology

Governance-Reviewed Parameters (Cyclical Review Permitted)

  • RM weighting coefficients (α, β, γ for TR²S components)
  • RE, PA, RI sensitivity factors for TR³S components
  • Capital efficiency gate thresholds (ROIC-WACC spread, FCF conversion, Net Debt/EBITDA)
  • CES tolerance ranges and associated vesting caps
  • Sector-specific baseline adjustments

Documentation Requirements

  • All parameters set ex ante before grant date
  • Changes to governance-reviewed parameters require formal board approval
  • No retroactive adjustments to vesting calculations
  • All changes documented and disclosed in remuneration framework updates
