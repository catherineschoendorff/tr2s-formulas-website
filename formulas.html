Extended Shareholder Return

TRS and Beyond:
Built for Transformation

Patient capital demands return logic that exceeds any CEO's tenure.

TSR Is Not a Strategy

Today's CEOs are expected to lead systemic transformations that take 7 to 10 years to mature, yet their performance is judged and their tenure often ends before the returns of those transformations can be seen or felt. That is a governance problem. Standard Total Return to Shareholders tells how the market responded and says nothing about whether the firm built or eroded the asset base that will generate future revenues and returns.

Two Lenses to Extend TRS

The first lens keeps Total Shareholder Return as the market outcome and adds an Asset Integrity Multiplier (AIM) that helps boards and reward teams see whether current returns come from preserving or drawing down the asset base that generates future revenues and returns. The second lens asks, for companies with direct extractive exposure: is the firm actively restoring the natural systems it depends on?

Philosophy of Value Creation

This approach goes further than the landmark Japanese Ito Review, the governance reform that called on institutional investors and boards to move beyond short-term market signals and redefined the philosophy of long-term corporate value creation. The Extended TRS lenses provide the architecture to act on that philosophy: a compensation structure that mathematically ties the CEO's long-term incentive to whether the asset base was preserved, and ensures that dividends are not paid on the back of mid- and long-term asset drawdown.

The CEO as Transformation Catalyst

1. The Starting Point: Market Reality and Capital Eligibility

Total Return to Shareholders remains the starting point. It tells the board where the market stands today. Before any transformation lens is applied, three financial gates confirm the company's eligibility to access external capital on favourable terms: ROIC exceeds WACC, free cash flow is positive, and leverage remains within acceptable bounds. These gates do not determine whether transformation happens. Transformation is not optional when resource scarcity reshapes the operating environment. The gates determine the cost and availability of capital to fund it.

2. The Transformation Objective: Preserving the Asset Base

The framework is business-model agnostic. Whether the company operates a linear, circular, or regenerative model is not the question. The question is whether the model preserves or rebuilds the productive capacity of the asset base under conditions of resource scarcity. Boards and CEOs must weigh the risks and adapt. The Strategic Asset Positioning Map provides the lens: one axis captures the scope of the model (product, business model, or ecosystem), the other captures the asset logic (extractive, preserving, or regenerating). A company's position on this map determines which return metric is appropriate and where the transformation journey stands. The board does not need to see every parameter. What the board sees is simple: Market Return, the Asset Viability Factor, and, where applicable, the System Conditions Factor. Behind these numbers, the reward team and board work together to assess resource origin, revenue model design, ecosystem orchestration, verified emission trajectories, and evidence of durable competitive advantage. These assessments determine eligibility for each lens and the weight it carries.

3. From Measurement to Compensation Architecture

With the two lenses in place, the board and reward team can design the CEO's long-term incentive. The Asset Viability Factor and, where applicable, the System Conditions Factor provide the adjustment mechanism. From here, the architecture follows: LTI target as a percentage of base salary, vesting schedules tied to the selected return metric, payout mechanics for vested shares, monetary value at vesting, and change-control rules that lock parameters ex ante.

What Practice Reveals

Good Intentions Are Not a Strategy. Nor Is Circularity.

When we examined companies across sectors, a pattern emerged that boards and CEOs must understand. Roughly half had invested heavily in circularity, achieving high recycled content, renewable energy, certified supply chains. The physical transformation was real. But many were destroying economic value in the process: commoditising their products, losing pricing power, or cannibalising their own revenue streams.

The other half produced ambitious sustainability reports and hit regulatory compliance targets while running business as usual underneath. Their return on invested capital hovered at or below the cost of capital. They wanted to look good. Their focus was not transformation.

The two lenses we introduced are agnostic. They confirm resource scarcity and measure whether the asset base is being preserved or drawn down. But when we applied them to actual cases, we found that asset preservation and capital stewardship alone do not guarantee competitive viability. A company can do everything "right" in physical terms and still land in a strategic dead end.

The Strategic Asset Positioning Map

To make these dynamics visible to boards, we developed a diagnostic tool: the Strategic Asset Positioning Map. The map has two axes that capture the two dimensions boards must evaluate together.

Strategic Asset Positioning Map

Capital Stewardship × Asset Viability × Structural Vulnerability

The horizontal axis measures Capital Stewardship: whether the company earns its cost of capital. It runs from high capital risk on the left (ROIC at or below WACC) to low capital risk on the right (ROIC exceeds WACC with a healthy spread). This is the Governance Gate. If you fail here, the extended lenses do not activate.

The vertical axis measures Economic Asset Viability: whether the asset base is being preserved or eroded. It runs from high asset risk at the bottom (commoditisation, pricing power loss, moat erosion) to low asset risk at the top (service-led models, ecosystem lock-in, durable competitive advantage).

Four Strategic Positions

The combination of these two axes creates four distinct positions. Each represents a different combination of capital discipline and asset viability.

PR Circularity Death Zone

High capital risk, high asset risk. These companies produce ambitious sustainability reports and hit regulatory compliance targets. But their return on invested capital hovers at or below the cost of capital. They want to look good. Their focus is not transformation. The framework assigns a Capital Eligibility Score of zero and blocks all extended metric bonuses.

Circularity Trap

Low capital risk, high asset risk. These companies have strong capital discipline. They may achieve recycled inputs, renewable energy, certified supply chains. The physical transformation is real. But they have inadvertently destroyed their economic moat. The product becomes a commodity. Physical circularity without economic asset preservation is dangerous.

Under-Priced Champions

High capital risk, low asset risk. These companies have strong asset viability. Their transformation logic is sound. But they are not yet earning their cost of capital, often because heavy investment cycles suppress short-term returns. They sit in a transitional state, waiting for capital discipline to catch up with asset quality.

Stewards of the Next Arena

Low capital risk, low asset risk. Both dimensions strong. This is the target quadrant. Companies here have secured their capital base and built durable asset viability. They are positioned for the next chapter: service-led models, ecosystem orchestration, and competitive advantage rooted in system logic.

The board does not need to see every parameter. What the board sees is simple: where does the company sit on this map, and what must change to move toward the top right?

From Closed-System to Open-System Strategy

Most strategy frameworks operate as closed systems. They ask: How do we coordinate better? How do we compete smarter? How do we capture more value within the existing economic logic?

Even frameworks that appear to embrace openness, such as platform economics, ecosystem orchestration, or AI-driven efficiency gains, often remain closed-system thinking. They optimise coordination, data flows, and competitive position within the current game. They reshuffle who wins. But they do not question whether the game itself remains viable.

This can be fatal.

When resource scarcity reshapes the operating environment, efficiency gains inside a closed system do not address the underlying crisis. You can perfectly optimise the engine while the fuel runs out. You can build the most efficient platform while the materials it depends on become unavailable or unaffordable.

Open-system strategy asks a different question: Does the game itself still have chips on the table?

It asks whether the underlying assets and systems, the physical resources, the natural systems, the supply chains, remain viable under conditions of scarcity and volatility. It asks whether the business model reflects the real boundaries of the system it operates within.

The two lenses we introduced are designed for this shift. They do not optimise within the existing game. They ask whether the asset base that generates future revenues and returns is being preserved or drawn down. They ask, for companies with direct extractive exposure, whether the firm is actively restoring the natural systems it depends on.

The Strategic Asset Positioning Map makes this visible. It shows boards where the company stands, not within the competitive landscape of the current game, but relative to the viability of the system itself.

Moving from closed-system to open-system strategy is not optional when resource constraints tighten. The only question is whether boards and CEOs make that shift before or after the market forces them to.

From Diagnosis to Action

The lenses diagnose. The map positions. But diagnosis without action is academic.

We have applied this framework to 20 companies across seven sectors. Each case illustrates a different combination of capital stewardship and asset viability. Some landed in the Circularity Trap. Some remain in the Death Zone. And some have moved toward the top right quadrant, building durable advantage through service-led models and ecosystem orchestration.
