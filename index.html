Capital Stewardship

TSR Construction, Capital Discipline, and Vesting Logic

1. Return Construction

1.1. TRS as the Baseline ▼

TRS remains the market reference. It captures realised shareholder outcomes and applies universally.

Total Return to Shareholders remains the baseline reference for financial performance. It anchors market accountability and comparability.

Total Return to Shareholders
$$TRS = \frac{(P_1 - P_0 + D)}{P_0} \times 100\%$$
Where:
  • P₁ = ending share price
  • P₀ = beginning share price
  • D = dividends paid during period
1.2. TR²S, Total Regenerative Return to Shareholders ▼

TR²S extends return interpretation to internal asset allocation, lifetime extension, and revenue structure. It applies across sectors and ownership models. Its focus is internal: how assets are configured, preserved, and redeployed over time.

TR²S extends return logic from pure market output to the structural quality of how assets are deployed, lifetimes extended, and revenue models made circular within the business.

Total Circular Return to Shareholders
$$TR^2S = TRS \times RM \quad \text{(when TRS} \geq 0\text{)}$$ $$TR^2S = TRS \times (1 - \theta \cdot RM) \quad \text{(when TRS} < 0\text{)}$$
Where:
  • RM = Regenerative Multiplier (asset allocation, lifetime extension, revenue structure effects)
When TRS ≥ or < 0 ▼

The Regenerative Multiplier (RM) amplifies upside and dampens downside.

When TRS ≥ 0

$$TR^2S = TRS \times RM$$

The Regenerative Multiplier acts as an amplifier. Internal regeneration creates additional value capture beyond market returns.

When TRS < 0

$$TR^2S = TRS \times (1 - \theta \cdot RM)$$

When market returns are negative, the downside damping coefficient (θ) limits the negative impact of the Regenerative Multiplier. This prevents compounding losses.

Where:
  • θ (theta) is the downside coefficient applied only when TRS is negative

RM - Regenerative Multiplier ▼

The Regenerative Multiplier is a weighted combination of three key operational metrics that reflect how well a company manages its assets, extends their productive life, and circulates revenue internally.

Regenerative Multiplier Formula
$$RM = \alpha \cdot RAR + \beta \cdot LVE + \gamma \cdot CRR$$
Components:
  • RAR (Regenerative Asset Ratio) = Regenerative Assets / Total Assets
  • LVE (Lifetime Value Extension) = Extended Lifetime / Baseline Lifetime
  • CRR (Circular Revenue Ratio) = Circular Revenue / Total Revenue
Weights:
  • α (alpha) = weight on RAR
  • β (beta) = weight on LVE
  • γ (gamma) = weight on CRR

1.3. TR³S, Total Regenerative & Restorative Return to Shareholders ▼

TR³S extends return interpretation to external ecosystem restoration and social regeneration. It measures how a company's operations contribute to environmental and social healing, beyond internal circular processes.

TR³S builds on TR²S by adding a restoration dimension: how does the business actively regenerate ecosystems and communities within its operating sphere?

Total Regenerative & Restorative Return to Shareholders
$$TR^3S = TR^2S \times (1 + RE)$$
Where:
  • RE = Restoration Effect (external ecosystem and social regeneration impact)

Restoration Effect (RE) Components

Restoration Effect Formula
$$RE = PA \times RI$$ $$RI = \lambda \cdot VCI + \mu \cdot ECR$$
Components:
  • PA (Purpose Alignment) = degree to which company strategy aligns with environmental/social restoration (-1 to +1)
  • VCI (Value Chain Impact) = direct regenerative impact within supply chain (0-1)
  • ECR (Ecosystem Restoration) = direct investment in ecosystem recovery outside supply chain (0-1)
Weights:
  • λ (lambda) = weight on VCI
  • μ (mu) = weight on ECR

2. Capital Eligibility

Extended return logic may inform long-term incentives only when the company is financially capable of sustaining asset quality and long-term investment. Capital eligibility therefore precedes all vesting calculations. It functions as a governance gate, not as a performance metric: if the gate is closed, extended return metrics (TR²S, TR³S) cannot be used in LTI.

2.1 The Free Gate – Capital Efficiency Gatekeepers ▼

Before extended return logic can inform compensation, governance must first establish that the company is financially sound. The Free Gate consists of three capital-efficiency gatekeepers that determine whether asset preservation is economically viable or whether the company is in financial distress. These gates are not performance targets but thresholds: they answer the question, "Is this company capable of investing in asset quality, or is it in survival mode?"

Gate 1: ROIC ≥ WACC ▼

This gate tests whether the company creates or destroys value from its capital base. Return on invested capital (ROIC) must exceed the weighted average cost of capital (WACC) by a governance-set margin; if ROIC ≤ WACC, capital is not rewarded adequately and asset preservation becomes secondary to financial stabilisation.

Calculation

ROIC (Return on Invested Capital)

$$ROIC = \frac{NOPAT}{Invested\ Capital}$$
Where: NOPAT = Net Operating Profit After Tax; Invested Capital = Total Assets - Current Liabilities

WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital)

$$WACC = \left(\frac{E}{V} \times R_e\right) + \left(\frac{D}{V} \times R_d \times (1 - T_c)\right)$$
Where: E/V = equity weight, Rₑ = cost of equity, D/V = debt weight, Rₔ = cost of debt, Tᵨ = corporate tax rate

Interpretation

ROIC exceeds WACC (Spread exceeds 0): Company is creating value. Capital is being deployed productively. Asset preservation investments are economically justified.

ROIC ≤ WACC (Spread ≤ 0): Company is destroying value or barely covering cost of capital. Asset preservation is secondary to financial stabilisation.

Gate 2: FCF Conversion – Free Cash Flow Supports Investment ▼

This gate tests whether the company generates enough cash to fund operations, debt service, dividends, and reinvestment. Free cash flow (FCF) must be structurally positive and convert earnings into cash at a healthy ratio; if FCF is negative or conversion is weak, cash is constrained and asset-preservation investments are deferred.

Calculation

Free Cash Flow (FCF)

$$FCF = Operating\ Cash\ Flow - Capital\ Expenditures$$
Where: Operating Cash Flow = cash from operations; Capital Expenditures = fixed asset investments

FCF Conversion Ratio

$$FCF\ Conversion = \frac{FCF}{Net\ Income}$$
Where: Healthy range 80-120% indicates sustainable earnings; below 50% signals cash stress

Interpretation

FCF exceeds 0 and Conversion 80–120%: Earnings are backed by cash. Company can fund operations and reinvestment. Gate 2 PASSED.

FCF ≤ 0 or Conversion less than 50%: Earnings are not converting to cash. Company is in cash stress. Asset preservation is deferred.

Gate 3: Leverage Ratio ▼

This gate tests whether the company's debt levels are sustainable. Net leverage must be within a governance-set sector cap; if leverage is excessive, debt reduction takes priority over asset preservation and regenerative investment.

Calculation

Net Debt / EBITDA

$$\frac{Net\ Debt}{EBITDA}$$
Where: Net Debt = Total Debt - Cash and Equivalents; EBITDA = Earnings Before Interest, Tax, Depreciation, Amortization

Measured over 3-year average or demonstrable trend; Sector-specific cap applied (e.g., 3.0x for most industries)

Interpretation

Net Debt/EBITDA ≤ Sector Cap (e.g., 3.0x): Leverage is sustainable. Company can service debt and invest in preservation. Gate 3 PASSED.

Net Debt/EBITDA exceeds Sector Cap: Leverage is excessive. Debt reduction takes priority. Asset preservation is deferred.

Summary: When All Gates Are Passed

Only when all three gates are passed is the company deemed financially free to allocate capital toward regenerative and restorative configurations.

2.2 Capital Eligibility Status (CES) ▼

CES condenses the Free Gate into a single status variable:

CES = 1

if all three capital efficiency gates are passed. Extended return metrics (TR²S, TR³S) are admissible as inputs into LTI vesting.

CES = 0

if one or more gates are failed. Extended return metrics are not used for LTI; payouts are either reduced to a distress cap or set to zero, according to the vesting rules in Section 4.

CES is determined mechanically from the three gates and carried forward into vesting mechanics as a binary switch.

2.3 Gate Threshold Disclosure ▼

All gate thresholds are set ex-ante and disclosed. For each gate, governance specifies:

  • The minimum ROIC–WACC spread required (for example, ROIC ≥ WACC + 2–3 percentage points).
  • The minimum acceptable range for FCF conversion and any temporary tolerance during investment cycles.
  • Sector-specific caps for Net Debt/EBITDA and any complementary leverage indicators used.

These parameters are governance choices, not optimisation outputs. Changes follow a formal decision process and are documented.

3. Compensation Architecture

Extended return interpretation matters only when it is reflected in long-term incentives. As long as long-term incentives are tied exclusively to traditional shareholder return, governance systematically favors timing effects over internal allocation quality.

3.1 Baseline Compensation Architecture ▼

The extended return framework does not redesign executive compensation. It keeps the underlying architecture intact and changes only the performance input used in the LTI formula. The following elements are treated as fixed inputs:

  • Base salary
  • Short-term incentive (STI) structure and metrics
  • Long-term incentive (LTI) target opportunity
  • Performance ladder form (0 / 50 / 100 / 150 / 200% of target)
  • Caps and floors on payouts

Extended return logic operates only through the choice of return metric that feeds the LTI.

These values are inputs, not outputs. The formulas below show how different return metrics convert the fixed LTI target into vested value.

3.2 Performance Ladder Logic ▼

The LTI performance ladder maps the achieved value of the chosen return metric to a payout factor. The standard form is:

  • Below threshold: 0% of target
  • Threshold: 50% of target
  • Target: 100% of target
  • Stretch: 150% of target
  • Cap: 200% of target

Interpolation between levels is linear unless governance specifies otherwise.

Performance Ladder:

These target levels are parameters. They can differ by metric to reflect their expected return distributions, while preserving the same 0 / 50 / 100 / 150 / 200% payout profile:

LTI Payout Formula:

$$LTI\ payout = LTI\ target \times performance\ factor$$

The performance factor is read from the ladder based on the achieved value of the selected return metric (TRS, TR²S, or TR³S).

3.3 Metric Selection for LTI ▼

Before any LTI payout is calculated, governance must fix the return input and the ladder parameters ex-ante.

Governance Decision Sequence:

  1. Select the return metric used for the performance cycle (TRS, TR2S, or TR3S).
  2. Confirm whether the standard 0 / 50 / 100 / 150 / 200% ladder applies unchanged.
  3. Set threshold, target, stretch and cap levels for the chosen metric.
  4. Decide whether TR3S is applicable for the cycle (based on structural extractive exposure).
  5. Define the point at which STI results are locked and no longer adjusted.

4. Vesting Mechanics

Vesting is the mechanism through which governance translates return signals into incentive outcomes. Grants are made at target. Final payouts depend on capital eligibility, return performance, and predefined vesting rules.

4.1 LTI Grant ▼

The long-term incentive target is expressed as a fixed percentage of base salary.

$$LTI\_target\ (EUR) = Base\ Salary \times LTI\_multiple$$

At grant, the target value is converted into a number of performance shares.

$$Shares\_granted = \frac{LTI\_target}{Share\ Price\ at\ Grant}$$

This anchors the incentive structure in equity math rather than abstract percentages.

4.2 Base Vesting Factor (BVF) ▼

The Base Vesting Factor reflects return performance under the metric selected by governance for the cycle.

$$BVF = g(Return\_metric)$$
Where: g() represents the performance ladder defined ex ante.

Typical ladder outcomes include:

BVF ∈ {0%, 50%, 100%, 150%, 200%}

Interpolation between ladder points is linear unless otherwise specified by governance.

4.3 Regenerative Adjustment (RA) ▼

The Regenerative Adjustment applies only when TR²S or TR³S is selected as the return metric.

$$RA = 1 + \lambda \times (RM - RM\_ref)$$
Where:
  • λ is a governance-set sensitivity parameter
  • RM_ref is the sector median or governance-defined baseline

RA is bounded within predefined limits.

$$RA\_min \leq RA \leq RA\_max$$

The regenerative adjustment refines the base vesting factor. It cannot override capital eligibility conditions.

4.4 Final Vesting Factor (FVF), with Price-Floor Governance Rule ▼

Price-Floor Governance Rule

Regardless of the calculated TRS value (which includes dividends), no vesting occurs if the share price at the end of the performance period is below the starting share price.

$$\text{If } P_1 < P_0: FVF = 0$$
Where:
  • P₁ = Ending share price at the end of the performance period
  • P₀ = Beginning share price at the start of the performance period

Rationale:

This governance filter ensures that executives do not receive LTI payouts when shareholder value (measured by share price) has declined, even if dividend income results in a technically positive TRS. It anchors vesting to capital appreciation rather than income distribution alone.

Application:

This rule is checked before applying the standard vesting formula. If the price-floor condition is triggered (P₁ < P₀), the final vesting factor is immediately set to 0%, and no further calculations are required.

Standard FVF Formula (applies only if P₁ ≥ P₀):

$$FVF = \min [ BVF \times RA \times CES\ ,\ Cap ]$$
Where:
  • BVF = Base Vesting Factor (derived from performance ladder)
  • RA = Regenerative Adjustment
  • CES = Capital Eligibility Status (0, 0.5, or 1)
  • Cap = LTI cap (e.g., 200% of target)

This single equation encodes the full governance logic.

Outcomes (if P₁ ≥ P₀):

If CES = 0

FVF = 0

If CES = 0.5

FVF is capped regardless of return performance

If CES = 1

Vesting proceeds without restriction under the selected return metric

If P₁ < P₀:

FVF = 0 for all return metrics (TRS, TR²S, TR³S), regardless of CES or calculated return value.

Example: Maersk 2024

Given:

• P₀ = 12,385 DKK

• P₁ = 11,905 DKK

• D = 1,120 DKK


Calculated TRS:

TRS = (11,905 − 12,385 + 1,120) / 12,385 = +5.2%


Governance Filter Applied:

P₁ (11,905) < P₀ (12,385) → FVF = 0%


Result:

Despite a positive TRS of +5.2% (due to dividend compensation), the price-floor governance rule triggers zero vesting because the share price declined.

5. LTI Payout

Once the Final Vesting Factor is determined, payout calculation is purely mechanical. No discretion applies.

5.1 Vested Shares ▼

The number of shares that vest is determined by applying the Final Vesting Factor to the originally granted shares.

$$vested\_shares = granted\_shares \times \frac{FVF}{100}$$

This calculation is deterministic once FVF is known. No adjustments or discretion apply.

5.2 Monetary Value at Vesting ▼

The monetary value of vested shares is calculated using the share price at the vesting date.

$$LTI\_payout\_value = vested\_shares \times P\_vesting$$

Where P_vesting is the share price on the vesting date. This is a mechanical calculation with no discretionary adjustments.

6. Governance Lock

All calculations are deterministic once governance decisions are taken ex ante. The framework distinguishes between fixed parameters and parameters subject to periodic governance review.

6.1 Change-Control Rules ▼

Fixed Parameters (No Changes During Grant Cycle)

  • Ladder structure (0%, 50%, 100%, 150%, 200%)
  • Performance cap (e.g., 200% of target)
  • Vesting schedule and cliff dates
  • Share price measurement methodology

Governance-Reviewed Parameters (Cyclical Review Permitted)

  • RM weighting coefficients (α, β, γ for TR²S components)
  • RE, PA, RI sensitivity factors (δ, ε for TR³S components)
  • Capital efficiency gate thresholds (ROIC-WACC spread, FCF conversion, Net Debt/EBITDA)
  • CES tolerance ranges and associated vesting caps
  • Sector-specific baseline adjustments

Documentation Requirements

  • All parameters set ex ante before grant date
  • Changes to governance-reviewed parameters require formal board approval
  • No retroactive adjustments to vesting calculations
  • All changes documented and disclosed in remuneration framework updates
