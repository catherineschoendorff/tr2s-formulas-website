TRS and Beyond: What Actually Creates Value

CEO, board and shareholder performance lenses to strengthen asset preservation and reshape the competitive arena.

The Governance Gap in Value Creation

CEOs are being measured on performance signals that do not capture whether they are building or consuming the firm's future. Traditional frameworks evaluate value creation primarily through market-adjusted Total Shareholder Return. Under conditions of severe natural resource constraints and system volatility, a firm can report strong short-term returns while simultaneously eroding the asset base and enabling systems that make future performance possible.

The Brutal Compression of Leadership Mandates

This tension is accelerating at a stunning pace. While average global CEO tenure has fallen to 7.1 years, the true pressure point lies in the sharp rise of short-term exits. Driven by activist pressure and demands for immediate transformation, an increasing number of CEO mandates are being compressed to just 3 to 3.5 years. CEOs are required to deliver rapid financial performance while making capital and infrastructure decisions whose economic consequences and resource dependencies extend far beyond their tenure.

The Structural Tension of Asset Economics

At the centre of this tension lies asset integrity and the ultimate viability of the company. Capital is committed, supply chains are configured, and assets are already in motion. They either remain productive across systems or begin to leak value through degradation, write-downs, and external capture. Early value creation is highly visible and rewarded by capital markets. Later value, residing inside assets, components, and materials, is harder to observe and easier to discount, leaving the condition of the asset base largely invisible to traditional financial reporting.

Arbitrating Between Viable and Non-Viable Futures

When Porter's classical value chain, optimised for efficiency within defined industry boundaries, remains technically feasible but becomes structurally unsustainable under natural resource constraints, preserving asset integrity shifts from an operational concern to a strategic constraint. It functions as the prerequisite for cash-flow stability, regulatory compliance, and access to critical inputs. Without lenses that showcase financially if and how value is being preserved or eroded, boards and reward committees lack the tools to incentivise decisions that secure the firm's long-term efficiency and viability.

Introducing Extended Shareholder Return

Extended shareholder return addresses this gap. It provides boards, reward committees, and shareholders with a mechanism to create space at the normative-strategic level for the CEO and executive teams. By retaining Total Shareholder Return as the external reference for realised performance, while introducing asset integrity as a governing condition, it allows CEOs to be explicitly rewarded for transforming the company toward a future-proof, efficient, and viable model. It distinguishes between returns that reinforce future earning capacity and those generated through asset drawdown. It also invites leaders to move beyond the level of products and services alone, toward new business models and ecosystem collaboration that reach beyond their respective industry boundaries to shape new competitive arenas.

This site explores that direction through specific examples and provides the ground to explore new potentials for CEOs and Boards.
